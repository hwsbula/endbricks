## INTRO BLOCK

*(Appears below title on cover or top of page 1)*

Most AI implementations do not fail because the technology was wrong. They fail because the process underneath it was not ready, the use case was not specific enough, or the team was not aligned before the build began.

This checklist is not a buyer's guide to AI tools. It is an operational readiness assessment — the set of questions that experienced practitioners work through before implementation begins. Work through it honestly. The "no" answers are the ones that matter.

---

## SECTION 1 — Process Readiness

*Is the workflow stable and documented enough to automate?*

> Note to designer: Section title is the large label. The italic line below it is a section descriptor — smaller, lighter weight.

- [ ] Can you describe the workflow step-by-step from memory, without consulting documentation?
- [ ] Does everyone who handles this process execute it the same way, or does it vary by person?
- [ ] Have you identified the specific step where time is lost or errors occur most frequently?
- [ ] Is the current process documented in writing, and is that documentation current and accurate?
- [ ] Has this workflow been stable for at least 90 days — without significant changes to the steps, tools, or team?
- [ ] Are the exceptions and edge cases in this workflow understood and accounted for, not just the happy path?

**What a "no" here means:** Automating a broken or inconsistent process compounds the problem. Process cleanup should come before implementation, not after.

---

## SECTION 2 — Use Case Clarity

*Is the problem specific enough to scope, build, and measure?*

- [ ] Can you describe the problem you want to solve in a single sentence — without using the word "AI"?
- [ ] Have you estimated the cost of this problem in real terms — hours per week, error rate, headcount involved?
- [ ] Is this one scoped problem or a cluster of related problems that have not yet been separated?
- [ ] Have you ruled out a simpler, non-AI solution to the same problem?
- [ ] Does this workflow happen frequently enough — in volume or regularity — to justify the implementation effort?
- [ ] Have you defined what the output of an AI system would look like, and who would consume it?

**What a "no" here means:** Vague problem statements produce vague solutions. If the use case is not specific, the implementation cannot be scoped or measured — and the pilot will almost certainly stall.

---

## SECTION 3 — Data and Systems

*Do the right inputs exist in an accessible, usable form?*

- [ ] Do you know exactly where the data required for this use case currently lives?
- [ ] Is that data in a machine-readable format, or does it exist primarily in documents, email threads, or verbal handoffs?
- [ ] Do you have the access rights and permissions needed to connect to the systems that hold the relevant data?
- [ ] Is the data consistently structured, or does it vary significantly between records, sources, or time periods?
- [ ] Have you identified where the AI output would need to land — the specific system or tool your team would actually use?
- [ ] Is there a clear integration path between your current systems, or does this require custom development?

**What a "no" here means:** Data gaps and access barriers are the most common cause of delayed AI pilots. Identifying them before scoping — not during build — saves significant time and cost.

---

## SECTION 4 — Stakeholder and Change Readiness

*Is there internal alignment on ownership, adoption, and change management?*

- [ ] Is there a named individual who is accountable for making this implementation succeed — not just sponsoring it?
- [ ] Does the team that will use the output have input into how it is designed?
- [ ] Have you identified who is likely to resist the change, and is there a plan for that?
- [ ] Is leadership aligned on the fact that AI implementation includes a training and adoption phase — not just a build phase?
- [ ] Is there capacity or budget allocated for change management, separate from the build budget?
- [ ] Has the purpose of this initiative been communicated clearly to the team that will be most affected by it?

**What a "no" here means:** Most AI implementation failures are organizational, not technical. Teams that skip this section consistently underestimate the cost of adoption — and overestimate how much the technology alone will drive change.

---

## SECTION 5 — Success Criteria

*Can you measure whether this worked?*

- [ ] Can you name the specific metric that should improve if this implementation works?
- [ ] Do you have a current baseline measurement for that metric?
- [ ] Have you set a target and a timeframe — for example, "reduce processing time by 30% within 90 days"?
- [ ] Is there a way to evaluate whether the AI implementation caused the result, or will it be confounded by other changes happening at the same time?
- [ ] Have you defined what a failed or underperforming implementation looks like — not just what success looks like?
- [ ] Is there a formal checkpoint in the plan — before full rollout — to evaluate results and decide whether to continue, adjust, or stop?

**What a "no" here means:** If you cannot define what success looks like before you build, you cannot evaluate whether you got there. Undefined success criteria also make it nearly impossible to secure internal continued investment.

## SCORING GUIDE

*(This block is visually distinct — consider a light background fill or a horizontal rule above)*

There is no scoring formula here. What matters is the pattern of your answers.

**If you answered "yes" to most items:** You are well-positioned to begin scoping an implementation. The remaining "no" answers are your pre-work list.

**If you answered "no" to several items across multiple sections:** Each "no" represents a gap that will surface during implementation — better to surface it now. Use this checklist to build your internal readiness plan before engaging a partner.

**If you answered "no" to most items in Sections 1 or 2:** Process clarity and use case definition should come before any implementation work. Starting without them typically leads to a failed pilot, not a delayed one.

A well-scoped AI implementation is a better investment than a fast one.

- The "What a 'no' here means" callout after each section should be visually secondary to the checklist items — smaller type, perhaps in a muted color or italics. It adds value but should not compete with the list.
- Do not repeat the full Endbricks descriptor on every page — once on cover, once in the footer of the last page is sufficient.
- The intro block can run on the cover (if it fits cleanly) or at the top of page 1. Do not split it across pages.
- Total item count: 30 checklist items across 5 sections. At standard PDF sizing, this fits in 3 pages with room for the cover. Aim for cover + 2 content pages + a closing page with the scoring guide and CTA, or consolidate to cover + 2 pages if layout allows.
- The asset should feel like it came from an experienced practitioner, not a marketing team. Restraint in design signals the same thing restraint in copy does.
